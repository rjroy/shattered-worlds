Theme Authoring Rules

How to define a world theme so it slots into the engine, tells the canon story, and reads as its own place. Derived from the shipped Zombie Big Box theme.

Status: current · 2026-06-10

◈ What a theme is

A theme is one worldId plus four coordinated artifacts that all key off that id.

LayerFileHolds
Narrativefiction, no fileThe Walker arrival, the world's catastrophe, the mythic Door
Datasrc/data/worlds/<worldId>.jsonCard templates + 3-act deck composition
Visualsrc/game/view/themes/<worldId>.tsPalette, frame colors, backdrop + door tints
WiringworldManifest.ts, themeManifest.ts, assetManifest.tsRegistration
Assetssrc/game/assets/themes/<worldId>/…Backdrop, intrusion overlay, cardfront, insets
RULE 0worldId is the single join key. It must be identical (kebab-case) in the JSON worldId field, the VisualTheme.worldId field, and every manifest entry. If they drift, selectTheme silently falls back to STARTER and buildWorld throws.

◈ The narrative spine

This is the constraint that makes every world feel like one game. Each theme is a variation on a fixed three-beat arc.

Beat I
Calm before
The player is in an ordinary place — a big-box store, an office building, a highway. Ambient unease creeps in.
Beat II
The intrusion
The Walker appears in the distance, not as a threat, and his arrival triggers the world's signature catastrophe.
Beat III
The Door
The Walker opens a mythic door — "Follow or meet your Destiny" — and walks through. Reach and clear it to escape.
N1The Walker is never the enemy and never themed. He and the Door are the constant across all worlds. They live in starter.json (shared The Walker, Summon Door, Door templates), so a theme JSON must not redefine them — the catalog throws on duplicate template ids. A theme only references The Walker in its final act.
N2The catastrophe is the theme's identity. Whatever the world's disaster is, it maps to the intrusion backdrop overlay, the signature threat-creature card, and the obstacle/ambient cards. Zombie swarm → airborne building → lava and ash.

◈ Deck structure

Composition lives under deckComposition.acts and is always three acts whose size curve expresses the threat-verb — not a fixed six-card-per-act rule. For example: zombie-big-box uses an escalating-bodies shape (5 / 7 / 9), highway-volcano uses a clog-constant shape (equal acts, pressure front-loaded), and bird-building uses an attrition-constant shape (equal acts, resources thinned over time). Whatever the curve, the three-beat arc must read clearly.

ActRoleContents (Zombie example)
1 — ForeshadowAmbient/sensory cards + minor obstacles only. No real threat yet.Strange Sounds ×2, Rubble ×2, Screams ×2
2 — EscalationIntroduce the signature threat creature in numbers, plus the tool-fetch that counters it.Rubble ×2, Zombie ×3, Find Baseball Bat ×1
3 — Climax + exitPeak threat density, ending on the fixed Walker closer.Find Baseball Bat ×1, Zombie ×4, The Walker ×1
D1Act 3 always ends with { "templateId": "The Walker", "count": 1 }. That is the fixed closer that spawns the Door: The Walker discarded → adds Door; cleared → grants Summon Door; Door cleared → SurviveWorld.
D2The starter deck is shared, not themed. starter.json provides the player's starterDeck; theme JSON provides only cardTemplates and deckComposition. The world manifest assembles [STARTER_SOURCE, THEME_SOURCE]. Reward player cards are where themes diverge: if two worlds grant an identical reward card, that is a rule violation, not a convenience. Every reward card must be distinct in effect, cost, or keyword interaction so that the player's deck fingerprint reflects which world they ran.

◈ Card archetypes

Every theme reuses the same mechanical roles, re-skinned to its fiction. This is the real recipe — map your new world's flavor onto these slots.

1 · Ambient foreshadow (world card, cheap, discardable)

Sets mood; on discard or clear it seeds a reaction card into the player deck. Zombie: Strange Sounds (clear → gain Listen), Screams (discard → gain Panic, clear → gain Regroup).

2 · Obstacle (world card, cheap)

Punishes ignoring it. Zombie: Rubble (discard → SkipDrawNextTurn).

3 · Signature threat creature (world card, costly, Creature+Slow)

The catastrophe embodied. Ongoing onEndOfTurn Damage, plus a discard-Damage penalty so you can't just dump it. Zombie: Zombie.

4 · Tool fetch (world card, Hidden)

Clearing it grants the weapon that counters the creature. Zombie: Find Baseball Bat → Baseball Bat (player card with a Creature bonus).

5 · Reaction player cards

Gained mid-run from archetypes 1–2, tuned to the threat. Zombie: Listen (Hidden bonus), Regroup (DestroyCardInHand).

6 · Signature player verb (one per world, exclusive)

Every world defines exactly one signature player verb — the concise phrase that captures how the player navigates that world's threat. No other world may claim the same slot. It is not a code field; it is the design invariant that keeps worlds mechanically distinct and prevents reward-card overlap. Assign it up front, before writing any card effects.

WorldSignature verbWhat it means for reward cards
zombie-big-boxsweep & noiseReward cards interact with Creature/Slow clusters; clearing many things at once
highway-volcanoeverything is fuelReward cards convert discards or exhaust triggers into resources

SV1The signature verb lives in design documentation, not in the JSON or engine. Enforce it at authorship time: if a new reward card's mechanical identity would fit another world's signature verb, redesign it.

Self-transform pattern (world card that degrades if ignored)

A world card that worsens when not dealt with spawns its successor and removes itself, both in onEndOfTurn:

"onEndOfTurn": {
  "kind": "Sequence",
  "steps": [
    { "kind": "AddWorldCardToTop", "template": "<worse-card>" },
    { "kind": "DestroySelf" }
  ]
}

The AddWorldCardToTop spawns the worse card onto the top of the world draw pile (it surfaces next turn), and DestroySelf removes the original from hand. The canonical example ships in zombie-big-box: a Corpse whose onEndOfTurn spawns a Zombie then fires DestroySelf.

Placement decision: this degrades onto the world draw pile (drawn next turn), not an in-place hand replacement. The threat resurfaces a turn later rather than persisting in front of the player.

C1Stay inside the existing effect vocabulary (src/core/model/types.ts): DealProgress, Draw, Heal, ReturnWorldCards, DestroyCardInHand, DiscardThenDraw, AddCard, AddWorldCardToTop, AddPlayerCardToTop, GainCard, GainEnergy, Modal, Sequence, Damage, SkipDrawNextTurn, SurviveWorld, DestroySelf, Brace (absorb snatches; persists across turns), DealProgressAll (apply progress to every world card in hand), ExileTopWorldCards (remove the top N world cards from the run entirely), DealProgressScaled (deal progress proportional to a run variable, e.g. remaining hand size — exclusive to overgrown-mall; no other world's theme JSON may use it), GainLight (grant Light, the fog-clearing resource — exclusive to fog-beach-party; no other world's theme JSON may use it), None. Adding a theme should not require new effect kinds beyond the list above. If a flavor truly needs one, that is a core change to flag separately, not a theme edit.
C1aDestroySelf removes the firing card from hand. Only meaningful in a world card's onEndOfTurn hook, where the engine knows which card is firing (it threads the card id as selfId). A no-op everywhere else (a played player card has already left the hand).
C2Keywords are the only five: Hidden, Creature, Slow, Spore, Concealed. Use them consistently so shared/starter cards still interact — e.g. Sprint's Slow bonus must still hit your threat creature, so your creature should carry Slow like Zombie does. Concealed is a numeric keyword: its value is the Light depth that hides the card (a card is concealed while its depth strictly exceeds the world's Light), so it is exclusive to fog-beach-party, the only world with a Light economy. Player card templates may also carry a keywords field (keywords were previously world-card-only). Don't invent keywords per theme without a core change. When reviewing a data batch for keyword saturation, expect only these five; the presence of any other string in a keywords array is a bug, not a new mechanic.
C2aKeywords are authored as strings in the JSON keywords array, in "Name" or "Name:N" form, and parsed at mint into a structured { name, value }. A bare name has no value ("Spore" → { name: "Spore" }); the numeric form attaches one ("Concealed:3" → { name: "Concealed", value: 3 }). The N must be numeric and the name must be one of the five above; either failing throws at parse time. Only Concealed currently uses a value — the rest are authored bare.
C3Every world card defines all three hooks (onDiscarded, onCleared, onEndOfTurn), using { "kind": "None" } for the ones that do nothing. Player cards define effect.
C4Player cards may carry an optional exhaust flag. It is not an effect, it is a boolean property on the card. exhaust: true destroys the card when played instead of sending it to the discard pile, so it never recycles into the deck. Defaults to false.
C4aPlayer cards and world cards may carry an optional canExile boolean property. Default: true. When false, the card cannot be removed from the run by exile effects — it always cycles back. The Walker and Door are the canonical false cases: they are load-bearing narrative cards and must not be discarded permanently by meta-progression exile abilities. This is intentionally a general capability — the engine treats it uniformly. There is no world-specific special-casing in the engine; exile resistance is a per-card flag, not a per-world rule.

◈ Visual identity

The VisualTheme interface is the contract. Required: worldId, intrusionHue, realityPalette (title/text/disabled/confirm/cancel), frameStyle (9 colors), backdrop (realityKey/intrusionKey). Optional: doorGlowTint, doorTint, worldCardfrontKey.

V1Each theme needs its own color identity. Pick a distinct intrusionHue and frame family per world (e.g. sky/feather blues for the bird, ember orange-red for the volcano) so worlds read differently at a glance.
V2Semantic colors keep their meaning across themes. Within frameStyle the roles are fixed even as hues shift: connectorDestroy reads as danger (warm/red), connectorReturn reads as cool/retreat (blue), ringAccent/connectorProgress pair with targetBorder. Re-tint per theme but preserve the warm-danger / cool-return logic.
V3intrusionHue is the theme's keynote and typically matches doorGlowTint. Pick it first; derive the rest.
Known debt zombie-big-box.ts is currently a near-verbatim copy of starter.ts (same green intrusion hue, same frame palette). That is a latent bug, not a model to copy. Giving Zombie Big Box its own palette is an open cleanup.

◈ Assets & wiring

W1Asset layout is by convention. Theme assets live under src/game/assets/themes/<worldId>/ with insets in …/<worldId>/insets/. You need at minimum a backdrop (<x>-reality.webp), intrusion-overlay.webp, a <x>-cardfront.webp, and one inset per themed card.
W2Inset keys are namespaced per theme to avoid collisions: zombie-inset-zombie, etc. A card's insetKey in JSON must have a matching entry in assetManifest.ts. Shared cards (Walker, Door, starter deck) use the un-namespaced inset-* keys already registered. Unregistered key → missing texture at runtime.
W3Three registrations per new theme, or it won't load:
  • assetManifest.ts — import every new .webp (backdrops, cardfront, each inset) and the <worldId>.json?url, keyed world-<worldId>.
  • worldManifest.ts — add a build<World>() that assembles [STARTER_SOURCE, <THEME>_SOURCE] and register it under worldId.
  • themeManifest.ts — add the VisualTheme to the themeManifest record. (STARTER is the fallback, not a registered entry; a theme must be in the record to be selectable.)

◈ Author's checklist

  1. Name it: pick worldId (e.g. bird-building, highway-volcano).
  2. Write the three-beat fiction: ordinary place → Walker + signature catastrophe → Door.
  3. Assign the signature player verb (slot 6); fill the five card archetypes with themed names/flavor; reuse existing effects + keywords.
  4. Lay out three acts whose size curve expresses the threat-verb; end act 3 with one The Walker.
  5. Pick intrusionHue, derive a distinct palette + frame family.
  6. Produce assets in the conventional folders with namespaced inset keys.
  7. Register in all three manifests.
  8. Verify: selectTheme(worldId) returns your theme (not STARTER), buildWorld(worldId) assembles without duplicate-template errors, and the test suite passes.

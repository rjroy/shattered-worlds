Shattered Worlds · Meta-Progression Design

Destiny should widen the game, not flatten it.

A practical design sheet for adding meta progression to a deck-building roguelite without making early worlds dependent on permanent power.

Core constraint

Every permanent unlock should create a new decision before or during a run. Avoid permanent numerical power until the base worlds are individually fair and complete.

Recommended phase

Phase 1: unlock variety, identity, and cross-world combinations.

Delay: permanent HP, permanent draw, permanent energy, flat damage bonuses, and grindable stat trees.

Progression options

System What it does Why it fits Balance risk
Starter Deck Evolution Unlock alternate versions of core starter cards and choose one per slot before a run. Lets the survivor begin each world with a slightly different survival posture. Low
Destiny Loadout Choose a small set of permanent memories, cards, or modifiers before entering a world. Turns meta progression into identity rather than raw power. Low–Medium
Door Fragments Equip relic-like fragments earned from completed worlds. The Door becomes the fiction layer for carrying rules between realities. Medium
World-Specific Starter Unlocks Cards earned in one world can appear in future starter loadouts. Makes worlds feed each other without requiring strict linear power escalation. Medium
Corruption A potential future mechanic where power, speed, or reward quality comes with reality-warping cost. Strong thematic fit, but should be deliberately introduced rather than assumed. High until scoped

Destiny Deck: three viable interpretations

1. Added to starter deck

Choose 2–3 Destiny cards before a run. They are shuffled into the initial deck.

  • Simple to teach.
  • Easy to implement.
  • Can distort early turns if cards are too strong.

2. Side deck / memory deck

Destiny cards sit outside the main deck and surface under specific rules.

  • More distinct identity.
  • Can feel like memories intruding into a world.
  • Adds another deck zone to explain.

3. Passive loadout

Destiny cards are not drawn. They modify starter cards, reward rules, or first actions.

  • Lowest cognitive overhead.
  • Best first milestone.
  • Less card-like unless visually presented as cards.

Best first implementation

Use a Destiny Loadout, not a literal second draw pile.

The player chooses three memories before a run. Each memory does one of three things: adds a card, modifies a starter card, or grants a narrow passive effect.

Memory of the Baseball Bat
Add Baseball Bat to your starter deck.
Memory of the Door
The first Explore each Act reveals one additional option.
Memory of Flight
The first Sprint each encounter returns to your hand.

Why not raw power?

Permanent power solves friction by reducing the need to understand the world. That is risky for Shattered Worlds because the worlds are supposed to be the content, not obstacles to grind past.

Avoid for now

  • Permanent max HP increases.
  • Permanent energy or hand-size increases.
  • Flat card damage upgrades.
  • Run-start bonuses that are always correct.

Suggested player loop

1. Enter WorldChoose a world and a small Destiny loadout.
2. Adapt DeckDraft cards that answer that world’s pressure.
3. SurviveWin through tactical deck use, not permanent stat advantage.
4. RememberEarn a memory, fragment, or starter variant.
5. RecombineBring cross-world options into the next run.

What survives the shattering?

Every meta-progression form is an answer to one question: what is allowed to persist when a world ends? The five systems above mostly answer "options." There are at least six other answers — and one we leave empty on purpose.

The empty one is power — numbers that persist. That's the "Why not raw power?" card above, and Principle 3's hard edge. Everything below persists something other than numbers.

Options persist

More choices before and during a run. Home of everything in the original table, plus Shard Depths and the Destiny Constellation.

Knowledge persists

The player's information state carries over. Home of Witness Knowledge: threats you have died to become legible.

People persist

Relationships accumulate. Home of The Waystation: survivors you rescue wait behind the Door.

The world persists

Consequences outlive the run. Home of Scarred Worlds and Echo of the Last Run.

Story persists

Meaning accumulates with zero balance surface. Home of the Survivor's Journal and Feats of Survival.

The challenge persists

Difficulty itself is the unlock. Home of The Ascent: earned harshness, earned rewards.

New forms

System What it does Why it fits Balance risk
Witness Knowledge Threats you have encountered (or died to) become legible in future runs: full rules face-up, spawn forecasts, a codex entry. Mastery transfer without power transfer. "The survivor remembers" made literal — even death earns something. Low
Feats of Survival Unlocks gated behind doing, not spending. "Clear a Zombie act without playing a 3-cost card" unlocks the quiet-build variant. Earning is the tutorial: each feat teaches the play pattern its unlock then rewards. Low
Shard Depths & Route Control Unlock world variants, remixed act layouts, and the right to see or pick the next world in the gauntlet. Content and agency unlocks — the safest kind. Principle 4: more ways to choose into the chaos. Low
Survivor's Journal Lore pages, world epilogues, card art restoration — earned by play, granting nothing mechanical. Pure retention with no balance surface, and portfolio-visible craft. Low
The Ascent Unlock self-imposed Burdens at the Door — harsher rules with better earnings, heat-style. Progression for players the base game stops challenging. Mechanically protects the summit Principle 3 defends editorially. Low
Destiny Constellation A star chart where lighting one star dims its neighbors; free respec between runs. Spending becomes identity choice, not accumulation. No "finished the tree" end-state. Low–Medium
Echo of the Last Run One card from your death deck appears in the next run's first reward offer. Failure feeds forward. Run-to-run, not permanent — it decays unless re-earned. Medium
The Waystation Survivors rescued mid-run populate the space behind the Door and staff pre-run services. Gives progression faces and a place — warmth the loadout screens can't carry alone. Medium
Pocket Stash Bank one small consumable across runs — what fits in your pockets through the Door. The smallest honest power carry, if we ever want one. Bounded by the fiction. Medium
Scarred Worlds Worlds remember runs: the barricade you built persists, hordes thin where you cleared, the Bird learns your tricks. The shattering becomes a history the player wrote, not a backdrop. High until scoped

Witness Knowledge, sketched

Nothing gets stronger; the dark gets smaller. Each threat has a witness state that upgrades on contact.

First Witness: the Walker
Die to the Walker once → its act-capstone rules are face-up from run start, forever.
Heard It Before
Encounter Strange Sounds three times → future runs show a forecast pip the turn before it spawns.
Codex of the Shattering
Every witnessed threat gets a Journal page. The codex is the bridge between this system and the Survivor's Journal.

This is the only form where dying is a clean, guilt-free earn. That matters for the feel of early losses.

Feats of Survival, sketched

The feat names the play pattern; the unlock rewards it. Spending currency never gates these.

Quiet Clear
Finish a Zombie Big Box act without playing a card costing 3+ → unlocks Suppress Panic starter variant.
Empty-Handed
Survive a Bird act with 3 or fewer non-starter cards in deck → unlocks Memory of Grip.
Outran It
Clear Highway Volcano without healing → unlocks Memory of Worn Boots.

Feats also solve the "what earns Starter Deck Evolution?" question better than play-count grinds — see the graveyard below.

Echo of the Last Run, sketched

  • Offered, never forced. The echo appears in the first reward offer; skipping it costs nothing.
  • One-run lifespan. The echo isn't permanent — take it again or lose it.
  • Arrives cracked. Slightly weakened, or exhausts on use, to stop best-card snowballing.
  • Never echoes starters. Only cards the dead run earned.

The fiction is free: the survivor carried one thing through the Door before it slammed.

The Ascent: Burdens at the Door

Each Burden is a named, equippable harshness with an earnings multiplier. Stacking is allowed; the multipliers are the bragging rights.

Heavier Door
One fewer option in each reward offer. +25% earnings.
Hungrier Worlds
Threat density +1 in every act. +40% earnings.
No Memories
Empty Destiny loadout. +50% earnings. The purist's run.

Grip and Window Seat assume the snatch-counterplay effects (Brace) proposed in shard-response-archetypes. If that proposal changes, these change with it.

Door Fragment instances

Fragments bend rules — information, rerolls, sequencing. They never add stats. Cap equipped slots at 1–2: each fragment is a permanent rules patch, and patches compound.

Peephole Glass
The top card of the world deck is always visible.
Worn Doorknob
Once per act, reroll a reward offer.
Welcome Mat
Hidden cards in the first act of each world are revealed.
Splintered Frame
When a world ends, carry one non-starter card into the next world's draft pool.
Deadbolt
Once per run, the Door holds: cancel one end-of-turn threat effect.
Deadbolt is the scary one — a once-per-run "no" can invalidate a world's capstone moment. Prototype last, if at all.

Corruption: three doors it could go through

The original table parked Corruption at "high risk until scoped." Here are three scopes. They are not compatible with each other — picking one is the scoping decision.

Door 1 — Corruption as cost-on-the-card

Corrupted Destiny options are strictly stronger versions of clean ones, but equipping any shuffles a Corrupted Memory — a dead or actively harmful card — into every world's deck that run.

Cleanest to build (it's just cards), easiest to explain, and the cost lives where the game lives: in the deck. Weakness: deck pollution is a familiar genre cost, low on wonder.

Door 2 — Corruption as the world's response

A meter, not a card. The more Destiny power the player equips, the harsher worlds spawn — threat density and clock speed scale with loadout weight.

This makes Principle 3 self-enforcing: the game mechanically refuses to let permanent power trivialize worlds, instead of the design team refusing editorially. Weakness: punishing players for using unlocks can read as the game resenting its own progression.

Door 3 — Corruption buys rule-breaking

Corruption is the currency for breaking roguelite law itself: continue a dead run, reroll the world order, re-enter a failed world. Every use permanently warps something in your Destiny.

The most thematically loaded version — the Door charges for what it should never allow. Also the most dangerous: "runs are disposable" is load-bearing in the vision, and a continue button, however priced, erodes it. Possibly a beautiful dead end.

Graveyard

Considered and rejected. Kept here so we don't re-litigate them by accident.

Banked gold across runs
Collapses the run economy — every run becomes farming for the next, and the current run stops mattering.
Play-count card mastery
"Play Sprint 50 times to unlock Dash Through" rewards time, not play. Feats earn the same unlocks while teaching something.
Prestige reset of the Destiny tree
Manufactures grind out of nothing. The vision says the possibility space grows; prestige shrinks it on a schedule.
Permanent stat tracks
HP / energy / draw ladders — already in the avoid list above, restated here as a corpse so it stays dead.

Open questions

  • What are "winnings," actually? The vision says spend your winnings — earned per world cleared? Per act? Per feat? One currency or one per axis?
  • Does the loadout itself grow? Going from 3 memory slots to 4 is the juiciest single unlock in the game. Which system grants it, and how late?
  • Seeds and scars. Persistent world state must be an input to run setup or "same seed + same actions = same run" breaks. Meta state as a seed ingredient is fine; mutating world definitions between runs is not. Scarred Worlds lives or dies on this.
  • Where does Witness state live? It's player profile data the renderer reads, not core run state — probably the cheapest system here precisely because it never touches the simulation.

A coherent first stack

If the question is "what ships together," one answer hangs together unusually well:

  • Destiny Loadout stays the spine (already the chosen first implementation above).
  • Feats of Survival are the earn mechanism — memories and starter variants are unlocked by doing, not buying.
  • Witness Knowledge comes free with dying, so the first ten losses all pay out something.
  • The Survivor's Journal wraps all three: feats, witnessed threats, and collected memories are pages in one book.

That stack needs no currency design at all — which defers the hardest open question instead of answering it badly. The Ascent and Shard Depths slot in later without reworking any of it.

Design thesis

The survivor is not becoming stronger. The survivor is carrying more of their Destiny across the shattering, one broken world to the next. Destiny progression should make each new run more expressive, not simply easier.

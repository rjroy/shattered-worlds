The World Deck as Acquisition Engine ● draft

The world deck holds the cards you gain

The twist that ties the design together. The world deck is not only the threat. It is also where the player acquires cards, and it is the run's pacing. One mechanism carries threat, economy, and structure at once. Builds on the single-currency model in Progress vs Hazard. Rules in this pass are resolved unless flagged.

The core idea

Every world card is dual-natured: a Hazard to answer and, often, a reward card to claim. Each turn you draw a mixed hand from both decks. For every world card in hand you make a three-way decision:

1. Resolve — pay its full Hazard cost in Progress this turn, claim its reward (often a card added to your deck), it leaves your hand.
2. Discard — take its penalty now, it leaves your hand. Legal at any time.
3. Hold — defer it. It stays in hand and eats into next turn's player draws.

That single decision is the whole game compressed: acquire (resolve), refuse (discard), or stall under load (hold). It is Principle 1 — the deck-building IS the game happening every turn, woven into the threat rather than bolted on as a reward screen.

▦ Why this is the strongest idea on the table

  • Acquisition is in the loop, not a side screen. The acquire / refuse tension the vision calls the most interesting thing on screen now happens under pressure, every turn.
  • Worlds become identity for free (Principle 2). A world defines both its threats and the cards it offers. The baseball bat (anti-Creature) exists because zombies do. A different world offers a different toolkit, so it forces a different build with no reskinning.
  • The synergy teaches itself (Principle 5). Zombies arrive carrying Creature, the store hides a Physical answer that reads for Creatures, and Hidden on that answer means your Explore cards help you find it. Set-up and pay-off are introduced in order, on the board.
  • Dead weight is now literal and live. Held Hazards starve your draw. That is Dominion's useful-dead-weight tension (research finding 3) made dynamic: holding for value costs you answers, so timing is everything.
  • Owned randomness (Principle 4). Acts give structured randomness: variety within an act, a fixed arc across them. Which cards you actually gain is your choice of what to resolve.

▦ What this supersedes from the earlier notes

  • The overflow board loss pressure becomes hand-clog: hold too many Hazards, draw too few answers, generate too little Progress, spiral. The HP track stays (the zombie penalty deals damage, Med Kit heals).
  • The curse-injection idea for dead weight is dropped. The world cards themselves are the live dead weight, and Panic is a normal player tool, not a curse.

▦ Worked example — the starter deck (10)

Ten mundane cards, the baseline every run begins from.

Sprint×2
Draw 2 player cards and draw 1 world card. Or +1 Progress to a Slow Hazard.
Explore×3
+1 Progress. Explore 1 (+1 Progress vs Hidden).
Barricade×2
+1 Progress. Return up to 2 world cards to the world deck (0 is valid).
Med Kit×1
+2 HP.
Panic×1
Return 1 world card to the world deck. Draw 1 world card.
Adrenaline×1
Discard 1 player card. Draw 2 cards.
Two return-vs-discard tools, distinct on purpose. Barricade returns cards (defer with no penalty, defensive) while still making Progress; Panic churns (return one, draw one). Returned cards shuffle into the top 3 of the world deck, so they come back soon. Neither claims a reward, so returning a card you wanted is its own opportunity cost. Discarding, by contrast, always eats the penalty.

The Walker is meant to be unwinnable the first time. At 10 hz with no Progress carry-over, the starter deck cannot clear it in one turn. That is the design. You discard it, its penalty forces a Door, you pay 2 to survive. Summon Door (player) — add Door to top of the world deck. Door (world) — 2 hz, penalty: cannot be discarded, reward: survive the world. You survive either way; the only difference is who summons the Door. You come back later with gear and a real engine to burst the 10 in a single turn. The Walker is the wall that sells meta-progression.

▦ Resolved rules (this pass)

No Progress carry-over

Progress is spent the turn it is made; any left over is lost at end of turn. A Hazard resolves only if you pay its full cost in a single turn. This is deliberate: it makes big Hazards demand a big single-turn burst, which is exactly what the x5 Progress-style multipliers exist to deliver (see Progress vs Hazard).

Thinning lives on Regroup

Resolving Screams grants Regroup — destroy up to 1 card in hand. Destroy is permanent removal, and because it targets the hand it can excise a clogging Hazard or a junk player card. That is the release valve the deck needs (research finding 3 — thinning is a core strategy).

Draw rules

Refill the hand toward 6 each turn, and always draw at least 1 new world card. Held world cards eat your player draws first:

World pressure never stops (minimum 1), so clogging starves your answers without sparing you the threat. Discard, resolve, Regroup, and return are how you dig out. Discard is always available, so the spiral is always escapable by choice, never a soft-lock.

Returns and destinations

Returned cards (Barricade, Panic) shuffle into the top 3 of the world deck — defer, not banish. Gained cards go to the player discard pile unless a card says otherwise (Summon Door says top of player deck).

Keyword tags settled

Hidden, Creature, Slow, Physical N, Explore N are all just tags a card reads for a Progress bonus, the single-currency way — no separate sub-currencies. Find Baseball Bat is now Hidden (you search the store for it), so Explore and the Listen reward help you afford it. That makes a clean chain: Listen → find the bat → bat beats Creatures → zombies are Creatures.

One assumption left to confirm. The draw-to-6 math assumes unplayed player cards discard at end of turn and only world cards persist in hand. That seems intended, but it has not been said out loud. Sign it off before it silently becomes a bug.

▦ The Zombie decision, as a flavor of why this works

A held Zombie is harmless (its 1 damage only fires on discard) but it clogs a draw slot. So each zombie is a live choice: spend 1 Progress to clear it cleanly, eat 1 damage to clear it free, or hold and choke your hand. A single Baseball Bat (Physical 3 vs Creatures) turns that math on its head, clearing zombies cheaply once you own it. That is set-up and pay-off, taught by the world, paid in one currency.

▦ What this confirms

Single currency was the right call and this leans on it: Physical 3, Explore 2, and a future x5 Progress all read against one number. No-carry-over plus the 10-hz Walker is what makes single-turn bursts matter, which is what makes the engine-building power fantasy matter. The acquisition-through-threat model is the part most worth protecting. With the rules above settled, this is close to spec-ready for a single-world vertical slice.

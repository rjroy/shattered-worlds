Deck Mechanics — Single Currency: Progress vs Hazard ● draft

One currency, not type-matching

A revision of the earlier answer to thoughts.html. The first pass proposed typed points and "match, then pay." That imported the ARPG damage-type model into a medium that can't carry it, so it's cut. The resolved direction is a single currency, the way Slay the Spire is really one axis (damage) wearing several costumes.

Resolved direction

One currency. The player generates Progress; the threat is Hazard. Every answer card produces Progress, every obstacle costs Progress and carries a clock. No type gate on the draw.

The next pass builds the simplest version of exactly this: single currency, single Hazard type, with optional Slay-the-Spire-style keywords (e.g. Explore) for light texture. Nothing more until it has been played.

▦ Why type-matching had to go

Damage types work in MMORPGs and ARPGs because you choose your loadout precisely and in advance: see the fire boss, equip fire resist. A deckbuilder gives that control at the deck level (what cards exist) but takes it away at the draw level (which cards appear this turn). A rule that requires a type match on draw gates you on the one thing the genre deliberately randomizes.

The killer case: draw a hand of all Search against a Chase obstacle and the player does nothing, loses tempo, takes damage, and none of it traces back to a choice they made. That is precisely the imposed randomness Principle 4 forbids. One scenario is enough to reject hard type gates. The "+2 Search or +1 universal" modal card we sketched was the tell: the "universal" branch existed only to undo the brick the gate created. When a mechanic needs an escape hatch stapled to every card, the mechanic is wrong.

▦ The currency: Progress vs Hazard

Slay the Spire is one axis. Poison is damage that repeats, Block is damage prevented, Strength is a damage multiplier. The depth is in rate, timing, and consistency of producing that one unit, not in matching a key to a lock (research finding 5 — simple parts, emergent depth). Adopt the same spine.

Model

Player cards generate Progress. Each Hazard has a cost in Progress and a timer: pay its cost before the timer runs out or it harms you. Because every card produces Progress, a weak draw is weaker, never dead. There are no bricked hands.

▦ Numbers go up — on purpose

Correcting the earlier pass: "numbers go up" is the goal, not a risk. The player should feel powerful, and feeling powerful means a well-built deck can erupt for 100 Progress in a turn off engine pieces and multiplier payoffs like "Followed the Walker. This turn, x5 to all Progress." That eruption is the reward for building well, and it is the same power fantasy Slay the Spire delivers within a run.

Recommendation

Both curves climb. In-run player Progress scales hard through set-ups and multiplier pay-offs (research finding 4 — set-up / pay-off / archetypes). The Hazard side scales right alongside it (costs and timers ramp across a world and a run) so the big numbers stay necessary rather than trivializing. The game is the race to stay ahead of the threat curve.

Separate the two scaling axes cleanly: in-run scaling is vertical and desired; only meta-progression (Destiny) stays lateral. That is the real meaning of Principle 3 — you cannot buy past the summit between runs, but within a run you absolutely get strong.

This is why single currency matters even more. "x5 to all Progress" is legible only because Progress is one number. Multipliers over a typed system would be a combinatorial mess to read and balance. The single-currency decision is what makes the power fantasy expressible — the two choices reinforce each other.

▦ Where the texture you liked actually lives

Dropping types does not flatten the game. The damage-type variety you enjoy moves off the draw and onto two places that can bear it.

1. Hazard shape

One currency, many threat behaviors, all priced in Progress:

  • Chase — short timer, cost climbs each turn. Forces burst.
  • Hazard (damaging) — bleeds HP every turn it stays open. Forces speed or healing.
  • Hidden — its cost is concealed until you commit or scout it. An information problem, not a type lock.

The player can always pour Progress at any of these. The skill is allocating finite Progress against differently shaped clocks.

2. Deck identity, plus light keywords

This is where the ARPG "pick your damage type" instinct belongs, because here you have full control: build a burst deck for a Chase-heavy world, a sustain deck for a damaging one. The world is telegraphed, the deck is your loadout, the draw only sets tempo within it.

For per-card flavor, use Slay-the-Spire-style keywords that grant a conditional bonus, never a gate. Example: Explore N means "deal N extra Progress to a Hidden Hazard." An Explore card is still useful against anything (it always makes its base Progress); it is just better against the thing it reads for. That is the StS pattern (a card that cares about a status) without a single dead hand.

Add keywords only where play feels flat. Ship the pure single currency first. Reach for an Explore-style bonus where a world plays one-note, not up front. Don't pay the complexity before the prototype asks for it (Principle 7 — prove fun first, then build rigor).

▦ Worlds still force different builds

Principle 2 needs worlds to demand a different build, not a reskin. The single-currency spine still delivers that: a world changes the shape and mix of its Hazards (and later, the rules of the Progress economy itself), which changes how you tune your deck. A Chase-dense world wants burst and thinning; a damaging-Hazard world wants sustain and healing. Same Progress-vs-Hazard skeleton, genuinely different build problem, no type-matching required. The loop stays the constant; the threat is the variable.

▦ The loss condition (still needed)

The original brainstorm said Hazards "overwhelm you" but never said how you lose. Without a fail state, triage has no teeth.

Recommendation

Two coupled pressures. Damage: each unresolved Hazard hits an HP track at end of turn; HP to zero ends the run. Overflow: the board holds a capped number of active Hazards; exceeding it is a loss or a heavy hit. Med Kit heals HP, which now has a clear job; Panic is genuinely dangerous because it pushes toward overflow.

Dead weight needs an entry path. A pure-downside card like Panic only exists if Hazards can inject junk into the player deck as a consequence of being left open (a curse mechanic). That is the Dominion / Slay the Spire lineage: useless cards that force timing and enable catch-up (research finding 3), and it ties the loss pressure back into deck quality.

▦ Still open

  • Does Progress carry over? Resolved: no. Progress is spent the turn it is made; a Hazard must be paid in full in one turn (world-deck note). This is what makes the 10-hz Walker demand a single-turn burst, and what gives the multipliers their job.
  • Hand vs Hazard draw ratio. 4 player / 2 Hazard is a guess and is the master difficulty dial. Instrument it from day one (Principle 6 — balance answers to data).
  • Timer shape. Does an open Hazard escalate its cost, its damage, or both? Each implies a different panic curve.
  • Where Destiny touches this. In-run scaling is vertical and desired; Destiny stays lateral — it unlocks new engine pieces and pay-offs and eases the early climb, but never grants permanent multipliers that let a patient player buy past the summit (Principle 3). Its own spec.
